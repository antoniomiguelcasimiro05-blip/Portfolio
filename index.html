Unit-Circle

Using the Unit Circle in Real Life

The unit circle is just a way of describing circular motion. Imagine a clock, a Ferris wheel, or even the Earth spinning. The circle helps us measure how far something has turned and where it is at any moment.

How to Find Points on the Unit Circle

To locate points on the unit circle, you start from the positive x-axis, which is 0° (or 0 radians). Move counterclockwise based on your angle θ. Each point you land on has coordinates (x, y), which represent (cosθ, sinθ). Since the radius of the unit circle is always 1, the equation x² + y² = 1 will always hold true for any point on the circle.

Unit Circle with Sine, Cosine, and Tangent

Any point on the unit circle has coordinates (x, y), which equal the trigonometric identities (cosθ, sinθ). For any angle θ measured from the positive x-axis, the endpoint of the radius represents the cosine and sine values: cosθ = x, sinθ = y. Using these, we find tangent as tanθ = sinθ / cosθ = y / x.

Notice that sinθ and cosθ always lie between -1 and 1, since the circle’s radius is 1. The unit circle completes a full rotation of 360° (or 2π radians). Along this circle, the axes divide it into four quadrants: 90°, 180°, 270°, and 360°. At 90° and 270°, cosθ = 0, making tanθ undefined.

Example: Find tan 45°.

Using the unit circle, sin 45° = 1/√2 and cos 45° = 1/√2. tan 45° = (1/√2) ÷ (1/√2) = 1.

Answer: tan 45° = 1

Pairwise Midpoints & Distances (Activity No. 5)

This activity is about plotting and finding the midpoints and distances between some famous landmarks in the Philippines using the Cartesian plane. I chose five landmarks from different parts of the country and used their coordinates to find how far they are from each other. It helped me see how math can be used to connect real locations.

Landmarks Used:

  • Rizal Monument (Manila)
  • Magellan’s Cross (Cebu)
  • Barasoain Church (Bulacan)
  • Fort Pilar (Zamboanga)
  • Banaue Rice Terraces (Ifugao)

Computed Midpoints and Distances:

Rizal Monument – Magellan’s Cross → Midpoint: (122.4407, 12.4378), Distance: 5.19° ≈ 576 km

Rizal Monument – Barasoain Church → Midpoint: (120.8956, 14.7110), Distance: 0.31° ≈ 34 km

Rizal Monument – Fort Pilar → Midpoint: (121.5247, 10.7488), Distance: 7.75° ≈ 860 km

Rizal Monument – Banaue Rice Terraces → Midpoint: (121.0585, 15.7561), Distance: 2.35° ≈ 261 km

Magellan’s Cross – Barasoain Church → Midpoint: (122.3569, 12.5662), Distance: 5.50° ≈ 610 km

Analysis and Reflection

Doing this made me realize how math can be used in mapping and geography. The farthest distance was between Rizal Monument and Fort Pilar, showing the wide gap between Luzon and Mindanao. The closest was between Rizal Monument and Barasoain Church, which are both important places in our history. Overall, it was cool to see how plotting points can show something real about our country’s layout.
